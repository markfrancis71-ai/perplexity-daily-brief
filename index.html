More than half of the loss ratio improvement in 2025 came from lower catastrophe losses (7.6 combined ratio points vs. 8.8 in 2024) and favorable prior-year reserve development (2.1 points vs. 0.7). Industry analysts warn that 2026's early tornado and hail season, moderating rate momentum, and elevated legal system costs signal that the pricing environment is already reversing—making AI-assisted underwriting precision and disciplined risk selection increasingly critical.

AI and P&C Insurance: Analysis Finds More Growth Than Disruption as AI Scales Across Industry

A ReSource Pro analysis published March 26 challenges fears of AI-driven insurance disintermediation, drawing on historical parallels with Internet-era predictions that failed to eliminate agents or underwriters—noting that independent agencies now handle 87% of commercial lines versus 83% a decade prior. On This Day · March 27

1513
Ponce de León Sights Florida

Spanish explorer Juan Ponce de León became the first European to sight the Florida peninsula, which he named 'La Florida' in honor of the Easter season ('Pascua Florida'). His discovery opened the door to Spanish exploration and eventual colonization of North America.

1625
Charles I Ascends the English Throne

Following the death of his father James I, Charles I became King of England, Scotland, and Ireland. His reign was marked by ongoing conflict with Parliament over royal authority, ultimately leading to the English Civil War and his execution in 1649.

1794
U.S. Navy Established

The U.S. Congress passed the Naval Act of 1794, authorizing the construction of six frigates and establishing a permanent United States Navy. The move was driven by the need to protect American merchant ships from Barbary pirates operating in the Mediterranean.

1814
Battle of Horseshoe Bend

U.S. forces under General Andrew Jackson decisively defeated the Creek Nation at the Battle of Horseshoe Bend in present-day Alabama, killing over 800 warriors. The victory effectively ended the Creek War and forced the Creek people to cede 23 million acres of land.

1871
First International Rugby Match

Scotland defeated England in Edinburgh at Raeburn Place in the first-ever international rugby football match, winning by one goal and one try to England's one try. The match established the tradition of international rugby competition that continues today.

1884
First Long-Distance Telephone Call

The first long-distance telephone call was successfully completed between Boston and New York City, a distance of roughly 235 miles. The achievement was a major milestone in communications technology and demonstrated the commercial potential of Alexander Graham Bell's invention.

1912
First Cherry Blossom Trees Planted in Washington D.C.

First Lady Helen Taft and the Viscountess Chinda, wife of the Japanese ambassador, planted the first two Yoshino cherry trees on the northern bank of the Potomac River in Washington, D.C. The planting, a gift from Japan, marked the beginning of what became the annual National Cherry Blossom Festival.

1964
Good Friday Earthquake Strikes Alaska

A magnitude 9.2 earthquake — the most powerful ever recorded in North American history — struck Southcentral Alaska, killing 125 people and causing catastrophic damage to Anchorage and surrounding communities. The quake also triggered tsunamis that caused destruction as far away as California and Oregon.

1977
Tenerife Airport Disaster

Two Boeing 747 airliners — a KLM and a Pan Am flight — collided on a fog-covered runway at Los Rodeos Airport in the Canary Islands, killing 583 people. It remains the deadliest accident in aviation history, caused by a combination of fog, miscommunication, and runway confusion.

1998
FDA Approves Viagra

The U.S. Food and Drug Administration approved sildenafil citrate (Viagra), manufactured by Pfizer, making it the first oral medication approved to treat erectile dysfunction. The drug became one of the best-selling pharmaceuticals in history, generating billions of dollars in annual revenue.
